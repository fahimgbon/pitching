Section 01

Pitching Is a Game.

And every game has rules.

I want you to stop thinking of pitching as 'presenting your work.' That framing makes you the subject. You're not. The other person is. Think of it as a game - strategy, moves, reading the room, optimizing for an outcome.
YOU
your pitch
?
THEM

Game Theory

The Game Has Two Players

Most people optimize for: looking smart

Winners optimize for: the reaction they need

Your job is not to tell the full story of your 11 days. Your job is to create clarity, trust, and momentum in the people evaluating you.

Here's the trap: you spent 11 days on this thing. You know every corner of your research, every trade-off you made, every feature you cut. The judges have been looking at teams for hours. They don't care about your journey. They care about: does this solve a real problem, does this team know what they're doing, and can I see this working? That's it.

Read The Room

Who is actually in that room?

🕐

The Time-Poor Senior

Skims. Wants the answer first, context second. Loses you in slide 3 if you haven't hooked them.

🔍

The Devil's Advocate

Wants to find the flaw. Not to be mean - to stress-test you. If you can't defend it, they can't champion it.

✨

The Vibes Judge

Decides in the first 30 seconds. Paying attention to your confidence, clarity, and whether you believe what you're saying.

You're not pitching to a monolith. You're playing to a room.

These three archetypes show up in every panel. Sometimes in the same person. The trick is: design your pitch so it works for all three simultaneously. Hook fast for the skim reader, have depth for the devil's advocate, and own the room for the vibes judge.
🔄

Judge Huddle: what loses you, what wins you?

With your table, build the list yourselves. Imagine it's 9 PM, you've heard 12 teams already, and Team 13 is walking up now.

What makes you tune out?

  • What first sentence immediately lowers your confidence?
  • What behaviour makes you think: they didn't rehearse?
  • What kind of slide or explanation loses the room fast?

What makes you lean forward?

  • What opener earns your attention in the first 15 seconds?
  • What proof point makes you trust a team quickly?
  • What makes you think: I want to hear the rest of this?
I want actual table talk here, not silent nodding. Build your own list. What loses you fast, and what earns your attention fast? When we come back, I want one tune-out trigger and one lean-forward move from a few tables, and then I'll show you the patterns that come up every time.

Common Failure Modes

Three things that kill a pitch before it starts:

01"Let me walk you through our process..." - No one asked. Lead with insight, not chronology.
02"We didn't have time to..." - Never apologize. Every team had the same time. Own what you built.
03Reading the slides word-for-word - You're not the teleprompter. You're the story.
I've heard all three of these in my first 30 seconds of listening to a pitch. Once I hear them, my bar is set. It's not impossible to recover, but you're fighting uphill. These three things signal 'we didn't rehearse' and 'we don't know our audience.' Both are fatal.
"What do I need this person to feel, think, and do - by the end of my last word?"

Answer that question first. Then build your pitch backwards from it.

Write this down or screenshot it. This is the filter for every decision you make. Should we include this slide? Does it move them toward that feeling/thought/action? No? Cut it. Does this opener get there faster? Yes? Use it. This question is the entire strategy for tonight.

A Framework for Pitching.

Not a formula. A checklist.

I want to give you a mental model. Not a rigid script - a checklist you can use to audit your pitch before you go up. This is PACLCOD. Bear with me on the acronym.

PACLCOD

P

Problem

State it in one sentence.
Make the pain feel real before you name the solution.

eventCRAFT example

Music festival-goers miss performances they'd love because current discovery tools weren't built for live, in-person chaos.

That's a felt pain: missed moments, information overload, and the difference between planning a festival and actually surviving one.

Your problem statement is the contract you make with the room. Everything after this is you fulfilling it. If the problem doesn't land, nothing else matters. It should be one sentence. Specific. Felt. Make them nod.

Persona snapshot

🎧
Amy | Festival Goer, 24

Uses Spotify constantly, saves dozens of artists, but struggles with set overlap and real-time schedule chaos.

Discovers music digitally Needs live coordination Travels with friends

PACLCOD

A

Audience

Who specifically has this problem?
Not "everyone" - a real person with a real context.
'Everyone has this problem' is the #1 red flag. It means you haven't done your research or you're scared to narrow down. Narrowing is strength. Specificity builds credibility.

PACLCOD

C

Context

What does the world look like before your product exists?
Market, behaviour, current workarounds.
Context shows you understand the space. You're not pitching in a vacuum. Briefly establish: what exists, why it's not enough, and what gap you spotted.

PACLCOD

L

Lead

Your hook. Your opening move.
Can be a stat, a story, a bold claim. Never "so our product is..."
❌ "So we built an app for music festivals..."
Tells me the category, not why I should care.
✅ "The average festival-goer misses 3 must-see acts per event. We built the fix."
Pain first. Curiosity second. Product third.
The lead is what you say in your first 15 seconds. It determines whether the judges are leaning forward or checking their phones. Craft it. Rehearse it. Don't improvise it.

PACLCOD

C

Claim

What is your product, in one sentence?
Feature ≠ claim. Outcome = claim.

Product job description

eventCRAFT personalizes your festival schedule in real-time using AI, AR, and collaborative crowd signals.

AI recommendations AR wayfinding Crowd signals

A judge should be able to repeat your claim back to someone else after hearing it once.

The claim is your product's job description. Not what it does technically - what it does for the user. Test it: can a non-PM understand it? Would a judge repeat it to a colleague? If yes, you've got a claim.

PACLCOD

O

Options / Evidence

Show your work. But fast.
3 slides max: data, prototype, user insight.
1
Metric

One number that validates the problem.

2
Feature

One demo moment that solves it clearly.

3
User signal

One quote, preference, or behavioural insight that proves resonance.

This is where most teams over-index. They dump everything. Pick your three strongest pieces of evidence - one number, one demo moment, one user quote. That's it. Everything else is backstory.

Competition close

We believe eventCRAFT is the right bet because it turns festival chaos into personalized confidence.

Leave them with your thesis, your key differentiator, and your confidence.

PACLCOD

D

Decision

What do you want them to do?
Every pitch needs an ask. In a competition: "We believe eventCRAFT is the right bet - here's why."
Competitions are a bit different from investor pitches where you have a literal ask. But you still need a close. Land on your differentiator and make it land with conviction. Don't trail off. End on a period, not a question mark.

💻

Show, don't tell.

In a 7-minute pitch, a 60-second live demo of your Lovable prototype is worth more than 4 slides of feature descriptions.

The prototype is not "nice to have." It is your most credible moment.

Your judges have heard hundreds of pitches where people described a product. Very few where they saw one work. Even a partial prototype that shows the core loop is more persuasive than perfect slides. Open with it. Or anchor around it. Use it.

Demo Discipline

The most common demo mistakes:

❌ Narrating what they can already see on screen ("And now I'm clicking...")
❌ Apologising for the prototype ("It's not fully built but...")
❌ Going to a screen no one can see
❌ No fallback if it breaks (screenshot, Loom, anything)

Demo tip: zoom to 150% before you start. Practise the exact click path. Don't freestyle.

Every one of these has happened to a team I've watched. The narration one is the most common - you're so in your own head you forget the judges can see it too. Talk about why what they're seeing matters, not what it is.

Section 04

The Slide Isn't the Pitch. You Are.

What you look like saying it matters as much as what you say.

Let's talk about the physical, embodied part of pitching. The part most people skip in prep.

Delivery

93%

Albert Mehrabian, 1967 - adapted for context

The Confidence Paradox

of communication is non-verbal and tonality. The words are only 7%.

You can have the best deck in the room and lose to someone who knows how to own a stage.

I know this stat is quoted everywhere. I'm using it because it's TRUE in a pitch context specifically. I've seen decks that were objectively better lose to a team that walked up with presence. Delivery is a skill. You can practise it.

Own The Room

Three Delivery Moves That Work

🎯

Pause Before Your Key Point

Silence signals importance. Don't rush past your strongest line.

👁️

Eye Contact Distribution

Sweep the room. Don't anchor on one friendly face. Find the skeptic and hold eye contact when you make your claim.

📣

Pace Drop at the Close

Slow down as you land your thesis. Speed signals anxiety. Slowness signals conviction.

Pause. Scan. Land.

These three are the highest-leverage physical moves I've seen shift a room. The pause especially. When you say something important, pause. Let it breathe. It tells the judges: this part matters. Pay attention.

Second Pitch

The Q&A is a second pitch.

🤔 "If you don't know, say: 'Great question - we flagged that as a risk. Our current thinking is X, and we'd validate with Y.'"
⚡ "Never say 'good question' - it sounds like you're stalling"
🎯 "Bring the answer back to your core thesis every time"
🤝 "Disagreement is okay. 'I hear you - our assumption is X because Y' is a great answer."
Q&A is where the devil's advocate wakes up. They're not trying to humiliate you - they're probing for confidence and intellectual honesty. The worst thing you can do is go defensive or make something up. If you don't know, your framework for finding out IS the answer.

Judge Psychology

The Four Questions Judges Are Secretly Asking

❓ Does this solve a real problem - or a problem they invented?

Ground it with evidence, not vibes.

❓ Do they understand who they're building for?

Specific users beat broad markets every time.

❓ Can I see this working in the real world?

The demo and evidence handle this.

❓ Do I trust this team to execute it?

Clarity, confidence, and honest Q&A answer this one.

Answer all four by the end of your pitch. Everything else is bonus.

Map every section of your pitch to one of these four questions. If a slide doesn't answer one of them - and you can't find which one - that slide probably shouldn't be there.

Pre-Flight

Before you present, run this:

✅Your problem is one sentence and specific
✅Your demo works and you've practiced the click path
✅You open with something other than "So our product is..."
✅Your close doesn't trail off - it lands on your thesis
✅You've said it out loud at least 3 times
✅You know your two strongest evidence points cold
✅You've thought about the skeptical judge and have an answer ready
✅You know what feeling you want them to leave with
Screenshot this. Run through it tomorrow before you go on. You don't need to be perfect on all 8. But you should know which ones you're weak on - and have a plan.
🔍

Audit your pitch with your team.

Open your current deck/notes. For each slide: which judge question does it answer? If it doesn't answer any of the four - it might not belong.

One output: the one slide or section you'd cut or restructure.

This is the hardest thing for any PM to do - kill your darlings. You worked hard on everything. But the best pitch is the most edited one. If you come back with one thing to cut or restructure, that's a win.
